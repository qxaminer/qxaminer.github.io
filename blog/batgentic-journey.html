BatGentic: A Late-Night Journey Through AI Bias, Time-Traveling Timestamps, and Interspecies Harmonica Duets

Or: How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love Bioacoustic Data Validation

September 2024 AI/ML Bioacoustics Data Science Research

The Setup: 1,168 Files and Infinite Optimism

It started innocently enough. I had 3.96GB of bat recordings from a single night with my Titley Chorus detector in the redwoods of California. The plan was simple: run some AI detection, identify species, create a sonification pipeline for my Data Expression course, and maybe impress the USGS folks I'm collaborating with.

What could go wrong?

Chapter 1: When AI Gets Its Geography Wrong

My first move was asking Claude for the "best" bat detection software. Like a good developer, I trusted the recommendation: BatDetect2, state-of-the-art deep learning, GitHub stars galore, excellent documentation.

I fed it my recordings and watched the M4 chip tear through 1,147 files like butter. The results came back with confidence: 86.8% certainty for Nyctalus leisleri (Leisler's bat). Beautiful work, really.

One small problem: Leisler's bats live in Europe. My recordings were from California.

Lesson #1: Even "state-of-the-art" AI can have geographic blind spots bigger than the Atlantic Ocean. Always check your training data geography before trusting species identifications.

Chapter 2: The Harmonica Plot Twist

While debugging the European bat situation, I mentioned to my bat expert friend that the recordings included some "acoustic contamination" - I'd been playing harmonica during the recording session and could see bats flying in as I played.

Turns out this wasn't a bug. It was a feature.

SonoBat analysis revealed about 50% of my files contained actual bat calls (the other 50% being harmonica noise). But here's the kicker: I had accidentally created a controlled experiment in human-bat acoustic interaction. The temporal correlation between harmonica frequencies and bat activity opened up an entirely new creative angle.

Lesson #2: Sometimes your "mistakes" are your most interesting data. Document everything, even (especially) when you think you've contaminated your dataset.

Chapter 3: Timestamp Chaos and the 1979 Problem

My USGS collaborator processed the same data through SonoBat and sent me the validation results. These results would tell me which of my 1,168 files actually contained bats versus noise.

The good news: Professional validation data identifying real bat calls.
The bad news: Half the timestamps said December 2024, the other half said January 1979.

Apparently detector clock drift, software timezone handling, and export metadata parsing don't play nicely together. Welcome to real-world bioacoustic research, where your data comes with a side of temporal paradox.

Lesson #3: Timestamp management in scientific software is where good data goes to die. Always plan for metadata chaos and build filename-based correlation systems.

Chapter 4: The Great Parser Wrestling Match

With validation data in hand (albeit temporally confused), I needed to extract which files contained real bat calls. The SonoBat export was a tab-separated format that looked simple enough:

Filename	HiF	LoF	Species	Manual ID	SppAccp	#Maj	#Accp...

Pandas couldn't parse it. Multiple attempts with different separators failed. The file structure was simultaneously tab-separated AND space-separated AND inconsistently formatted.

Finally, I gave up on elegant solutions:

with open('validation_file.txt', 'r') as f:
    lines = f.readlines()
bat_files = []
for line in lines[1:]:
    parts = line.strip().split('\t')
    if len(parts) > 6 and parts[6] not in ['', 'Inf']:
        bat_files.append((parts[0], parts[6]))

Sometimes brute force wins. Out of 474 analyzed files, 219 contained validated bat calls - a 46% hit rate that aligned perfectly with the SonoBat "half noise, half bats" estimate.

Lesson #4: When elegant parsing fails, embrace the crude solution that works. Perfect is the enemy of functional at 11 PM.

Chapter 5: The Cursor Productivity Explosion

With validated data finally in hand, I needed to build an interactive tool for exploring these bat calls. Enter Cursor AI, which took my rambling prompt and generated a Streamlit app that could:

  • Parse SonoBat validation files
  • Copy validated audio files to clean directories
  • Generate human-audible versions using pitch shifting
  • Create interactive playback with original + processed audio
  • Display validation metadata

9 out of 10 TODO items completed in a couple hours. The remaining item (probably audio visualization) could wait for tomorrow's energy.

Lesson #5: AI-assisted development shines when you know exactly what you want but don't want to write the boilerplate. Having real requirements beats vague feature wishes.

The Unexpected Takeaways

What started as a simple "detect bat species" task turned into something much more interesting:

On AI Model Validation

BatDetect2's European bias wasn't a failure - it was a discovery. It highlighted why initiatives like NABat (North American Bat Monitoring Program) exist and why regional training data matters. This "problem" actually strengthened my case for collaborating with USGS on geographically-aware models.

On Research Serendipity

The harmonica-bat interaction wasn't planned, but it became the most interesting aspect of the data. Real research often comes from following unexpected threads rather than executing perfect experimental designs.

On Technical Debt in Science

Every tool choice creates downstream consequences. BatDetect2's geographic limitations, SonoBat's timestamp chaos, and file format inconsistencies all represent the messy reality of scientific software. Building for extensibility and validation from day one pays dividends.

On Creative + Scientific Integration

The final BatGentic tool bridges rigorous scientific validation with creative exploration. Users can click to hear bat calls in both ultrasonic and human-audible ranges, exploring the same data from multiple perspectives. Science and art don't have to be separate workflows.

What's Next

The validated dataset is ready for creative sonification. The harmonica-bat interaction patterns are documented. The comparative study framework (BatDetect2 vs SonoBat vs future tools) is established. And most importantly, I have a working pipeline that handles real-world data messiness.

Tomorrow's goals: turn 219 validated California bat calls into music, document the geographic bias findings for the research community, and maybe get more than 4 hours of sleep.

But tonight, I'm calling it a win. Sometimes the best code comes from embracing the chaos, following the tangents, and building tools that work with reality instead of fighting it.

BatGentic is open source and designed for bat researchers, acoustic ecologists, and anyone interested in the intersection of AI validation and creative data expression. Because someone should build tools that make both scientists and artists happy.
